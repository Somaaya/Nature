Every living thing has its own ecosystem. The ecosystem is the natural habitat of these organisms, where their basic needs of food, water, and shelter are met to protect them from climatic conditions and survive. All living things need to adapt to their environment to be able to live. Adaptation is the change that occurs in the body or behavior of an organism to help it survive in its environment. Wild animals can only live in places that they are adapted to.

    Characteristics of wild animals:

  • Living in the wild: An animal is classified as wild when its natural habitat is the wilderness.
  • Untamable: An animal is classified as wild when it is self-reliant in finding its home and obtaining its food to survive, and does not interact with humans.

    The importance of wild animals:

  • Wildlife is one of the most important pillars of tourism, as people are attracted to the beauty and life of wild animals, which has a positive impact on the economy, creates new jobs, and has a positive impact on the environment and the preservation of these animals.
  • Historically, wildlife has played a major role in the daily lives of many cultures; as part of religious ceremonies, community events, and community bonding, wildlife still plays a major role in many third world countries.
  • Wildlife migration patterns, distribution and behaviour can be a vital indicator of ecosystem health and the deeper impacts of climate change.
  • Scientists can use animal distribution models to determine the best ways to conserve the natural environment.

    Facts about wild animals:

  • According to the US National Academy of Sciences, 4% of the world's mammals are wild animals.
  • An average of 96 elephants are killed for ivory in Africa every day, according to the Wildlife Conservation Society, and more are killed than are born; more than 60% die as a result of poaching.
  • Biodiversity in Latin America has declined by about 83% since the 1970s, according to Defenders of Wildlife.
  • The number of lions in Africa has declined by about 42% over the past two decades, according to the African Wildlife Foundation.
